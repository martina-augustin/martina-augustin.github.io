    Data visualisation. Presenting origin-destination data.

    At the onset of the Covid-19 pandemic in early 2020, many workers transitioned to working from home, whilst others had to commute by private means, for fear of being contaminated in public transport. The question was, what would happen once the lockdown ended and everyone would return to their workplace. More importantly, what would happen if majority of workers who used to commute by public transport would then switch to private cars?

    Goal

    The goal was to analyse, visualise and to help understand which roads would be the most affected by commuters switching from public transport to private cars.

    Process

    I used an exploratory geo-spatial data analysis to identify areas with the highest commuting flows, based on varying distances and commuting methods. The data used in the analysis came from the Census 2011 as the most complete information on commuting patterns at the time of analysis. The spatial dimension of the set was included through local authority district Shapefiles which I crossed with the Census data. These formed the base for creating the maps and Sankey diagrams, which were all produced using ggplot and ggforce packages in R.

    Result

    The map of commuters in England shows different means of commuting patterns based on shorter and longer distances. There appears to be relatively low number of commuters using public transport, compared to private vehicle users, which could signalise that those changing from public transportation to private would not cause a great disruption on the roads.

    A more detailed view is produced for specific areas of England, in this example the area of London. I chose to show all local authority districts within 60km from central London. The map highlights the districts that have the highest numbers of public transport commuters, which could translate into increased road traffic if the users decided to change the means of commuting.

    The Sankey diagrams below go even further into detail and display the actual commuter numbers for all travels into the central London districts (for sake of simplicity hereafter, I only considered the districts between which the number of trips was above 1000). This information can be simply presented in a table, but using the Sankey plots brings an additional value that can help quickly identify high commuter flows. It is therefore a great option to use alongside any structured tabular origin-destination data.
